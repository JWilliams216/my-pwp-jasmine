Children

Spending quality time with children can be challenging, especially with busy schedules and the demands of daily life. However, with some planning and creativity, it is possible to make the most of the time you have with your children.

Bedtime Routine
  • Set a consistent bedtime: regulate their inner clock
  • Wind down: 15-30 minutes prior
  • Avoid stimulating activities: No roughhousing or screen time
  • Dim lights: creating a calm and restful atmosphere
  • Establish a sleep-friendly environment: dark, quiet, white noise
  • Limit food and drink: especially sugar and large meals
  • Bath or shower: calming their body
  • Read and cuddle

Household

Schedule

  • Box Method: It's flexible and can be adjusted to fit each day.
  • Use Google Keep or time blocking apps.
  • Box off when you wake up and go to sleep.
  • Box fixed commitments: When you go to work, kids go to school or daycare, and appointments.
  • Box high-priority tasks: Things that need to be done that day that are most important to your goals and responsibilities.
  • Box high-priority tasks: Tasks or activities that you enjoy, such as reading, exercise, time with friends.

Organizing

  • Break into small manageable steps.
  • De-clutter: Go through each room removing items you no longer need or belong in other rooms.
  • Return items to designated place.
  • Use labeled storage solutions: Shelves, baskets, drawers.
  • Keep surfaces clear: Tabletops, counters, and tops of dressers.

Commitments

  • Make a list of all your commitments, no matter how small: Work, personal, and family obligations.
  • Decided which commitments you can eliminate: It's okay to say no and not be able to fulfill all commitments that come across your plate.
  • Prioritize with numbers: Which need to happen that day and which can be done within the week.
  • Break up larger commitments into smaller more manageable tasks.
  • Delegate when possible.

Chores

  • Make a list of all chores.
  • Break larger chores into smaller tasks if needed.
  • Determine how often each chore needs to be done: Daily, weekly, monthly, or a couple of times a year.
  • Figure out how long it takes to accomplish each chore: Time yourself doing each chore and keep a record.
  • Assign each chore to a specific day or time of week.
  • Use a chore chart or add to your calendar in different color to keep yourself on track throughout the week.
Mental Health & Self-Care

Support & Community

Your support network can include anyone who provides emotional, practical, or professional support, such as family, friends, coworkers, mental health professionals, support groups, faith-based or community organizations, and non-profits. The key factor that defines someone as part of your support network is that they offer you support, advice, and a listening ear when you need it, helping you manage stress, cope with challenges, and improve your well-being. Building and maintaining a support network that meets your specific needs and preferences involves reaching out to existing relationships, seeking new connections, and actively engaging with your support network to develop strong and meaningful relationships.

Peer-led Support Groups

Peer-led support groups are run by individuals who have experienced mental health issues themselves. These groups provide a supportive environment where members can share their experiences, provide emotional support, and learn from each other.

Online Support Groups

Online support groups provide a virtual space for individuals to connect with others who are experiencing similar mental health challenges. These groups can be helpful for individuals who are unable to attend in-person support groups or who prefer the anonymity of an online community.

Specific Mental Condition Group

Support groups exist for people with various mental health conditions like depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia. These groups offer a secure environment for individuals to exchange their experiences, obtain coping skills, and get emotional support from others who relate to their struggles.

Faith-based Support Groups

Many faith-based organizations offer support groups for individuals struggling with mental health challenges. These groups provide a supportive environment where individuals can receive emotional support and guidance based on their faith.

Moodfit

Provides a mood tracker, guided meditations, and goal setting features to help individuals manage stress, anxiety, and depression.

Mental Health America (MHA)

MHA is a non-profit organization that promotes mental health and provides education, advocacy, and support to individuals affected by mental illness. Their website offers information on mental health conditions, treatment options, and resources for support

American Psychological Association (APA)

The APA is a professional organization for psychologists that provides resources and support for mental health professionals and individuals seeking mental health support. Their website offers information on mental health conditions, treatment options, and resources for support.

National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)

NIMH is a government agency that conducts research on mental health and provides information and resources for individuals affected by mental illness. Their website offers information on mental health conditions, treatment options, and resources for support.

HelpGuide

Provides educational articles and resources for various mental health conditions, including depression, anxiety, and PTSD.

National Suicide Prevention Lifeline

The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline provides free and confidential support 24/7 to individuals who are in crisis or feeling suicidal.

Crisis Text Line

Crisis Text Line provides free and confidential support via text message 24/7 to individuals who are in crisis or feeling suicidal.

SAMHSA National Helpline

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) National Helpline provides free and confidential support 24/7 to individuals facing mental health or substance abuse issues

National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) Helpline

The NAMI Helpline provides free and confidential support to individuals and families affected by mental illness.

Pediatrician

  • Are there any lifestyle changes or self-care strategies that can improve my child's mental health and reduce stress?
  • How can I support my child's emotional well-being and foster a positive mental health environment at home?
  • When should I seek professional help for my child's mental health, and what treatment options are available?

Accident & Emergency Medicine

  • Are there any immediate treatment options available for mental health conditions, such as medication or crisis counseling?

In-person therapy.

This is the traditional way of therapy where you meet with a therapist in their office or a clinical setting. In-person therapy allows for face-to-face communication, which can help build rapport and trust between the therapist and client

Teletherapy

Teletherapy is a type of remote therapy where you communicate with a therapist through video chat or phone calls. It's convenient as you can attend sessions from anywhere with an internet connection, making it an excellent choice for people with mobility issues, those living in remote areas, or those who struggle to leave their homes.

Group therapy

Group therapy involves meeting with a therapist and a group of individuals who are experiencing similar issues. Group therapy can be a good option for those who benefit from peer support and can also be more affordable than individual therapy.

Text-based Therapy

Some therapists offer therapy sessions through text-based communication, such as messaging or email. This can be a good option for those who prefer to communicate in writing or who have difficulty expressing themselves verbally.
